Exploratory Analysis --- Identify Strong Attributes

Temperature

Every 1F temperature increase brings up the total number of red light and speeding violations by 8 and 7, respectively. Putting these numbers into context: if we approximate the temperature change from day to day is around 10 - 20 F. The total number for red light and speeding violations could change by 100 - 200. It is roughly 2% - 3% change, comparing with the average number of daily violations.

Precipitation

The precipitation reported here is defined as the sum of rain and melted snow/ice. Every 1 inch precipitation increase brings down the total number of red light and speeding violations by 70 and 750, respectively. The speeding violation dependence on precipitation is about 5 - 6 times stronger than the one for the red light violations. If a heavy rain brings 1 inch of precipitation, it decreases the speeding and red light violations by 25% and 5%, compared with days with no precipitation.

Weekday

Fun facts Red light data: people are more careless on Saturdays, and more carefull on Mondays. Speeding data: people with bad adventure habit are more excited on Fridays and less excited on Sundays. The weekday dependence is stronger for red light violations (Sat violations are 27% more than Mon violations), and weaker for speeding violations (Fri violations are 15% more than Sun violations).

Location

The location of cameras appears to be a strong feature determining the number of violations. The crash rate varies heavily from one location to another.

Machine learning

Input Features

For each location, the average number of daily violations over all historical data contained in the training set was included as an feature. Additionally, I also used daily-weather-related features, including highest temperature, lowest temperature, rain/snow precipitation, accumulated snow depth on the ground.

Model Selection

Since the location attribute appears strongly influencing the output, I use the mean model results as the benchmark. I applied various models, including linear regression, k-nearest neighbor, and random forests. In general, RF method performs better in terms of forecasting violation risks.

Accuracy

The data set is split into the training and test data sets, which contain 80% and 20% of the whole data. I applied cross validation to measure the prediction error. Comparing with the mean model, The mean-squared-error (MSE) values of the random forest method are 89% and 85% for red light and speeding violations, respectively.

Web App

An interactive web app can be found on the Predictor tab. Top ten violation locations for both red light and speeding are predicted based on the input weather condition.
